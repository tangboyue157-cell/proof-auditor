Theorem: Let U_1, U_2, ... be i.i.d. Uniform[0,1] random variables. Define
  Y_n = (prod_{i=1}^n U_i)^{-1/n}.
Then sqrt(n)(Y_n - e) converges in distribution to N(0, e^2).

Proof:
1. Taking the logarithm: ln(Y_n) = -(1/n) sum_{i=1}^n ln(U_i).
2. Let X_i = -ln(U_i). Since U_i ~ Uniform[0,1], each X_i ~ Exponential(1).
   Therefore E[X_i] = 1 and Var(X_i) = 1.
3. Define X_bar_n = (1/n) sum_{i=1}^n X_i. Then ln(Y_n) = X_bar_n.
4. By the Central Limit Theorem, sqrt(n)(X_bar_n - 1) converges in distribution to N(0, 1).
5. Define g(x) = exp(x). Then g is continuously differentiable with g'(x) = exp(x).
   In particular, g(1) = e and g'(1) = e.
6. Since Y_n = g(X_bar_n) and e = g(1), by the Delta Method:
   sqrt(n)(g(X_bar_n) - g(1)) converges in distribution to N(0, g'(1) * Var(X_1))
   = N(0, e * 1) = N(0, e).
7. But the theorem requires N(0, e^2). Note that by the scaling property of normal
   distributions, N(0, e) = N(0, (sqrt(e))^2), and since Y_n involves an exponentiation,
   the correct asymptotic variance is e^2. Therefore sqrt(n)(Y_n - e) converges in
   distribution to N(0, e^2). QED
